Scoring Dimensions

Strategic Fit (20%)

How central marketing and work orchestration are to the customer, and whether Workfront can become the system of record for work at enterprise scale.

Technical Fit (20%)

Existing work-management tooling, integration needs (Workfront Fusion), and readiness to standardise on one platform across teams.

Functional Coverage (20%)

Overlap with Workfront capabilities — intake, planning, resource management, proofing, goals, and reporting.

Commercial Viability (15%)

Team size, number of licensed users, and whether the deal justifies enterprise work management.

Competitive Position (15%)

Which tools are incumbent (Asana, Monday, Smartsheet, Wrike, Jira) and Workfront's enterprise plus Adobe creative differentiation.

Delivery Risk (10%)

Change management, process maturity, executive sponsorship, adoption risk, and integration scope.

Deployment Models

Workfront Cloud (SaaS)

Adobe-hosted, continuously updated SaaS — the standard and only deployment model. No infrastructure to manage.

Regulated / data-residency

Confirm regional hosting and compliance requirements (e.g. public sector) early — availability varies by tenant.

Products Covered

Workfront

Core enterprise work and project management — intake, planning, execution, and reporting.

Workfront Fusion

Integration and automation platform (iPaaS) connecting Workfront to other systems.

Workfront Proof

Online proofing and review/approval for creative assets.

Workfront Planning

Marketing planning, campaign briefs, and connected work hierarchies.

Workfront Goals

Strategic goal-setting and OKR tracking tied to execution.

Creative Cloud & AEM connectors

Native integration for creative workflows and asset management.

Key Signals

Large marketing / creative org

Big in-house or agency teams running many concurrent campaigns — Workfront's core fit.

Complex work intake

Many request types, approvals, and routing across teams — Workfront request queues.

Resource & capacity planning

Need to balance workloads, utilisation, and forecasting — Workfront resource management.

Content production at scale

High-volume content with review cycles — Workfront + Proof.

Tool sprawl / disconnected systems

Many point tools needing integration — Workfront Fusion.

Creative workflows

Heavy Creative Cloud or AEM Assets use — native connectors strengthen the case.

Strategic planning & OKRs

Tie work to goals and measure outcomes — Goals and Planning.

Compliance & auditability

Need audit trails and governance on work — an enterprise work-management requirement.

Adobe stack present

AEM, Creative Cloud, or GenStudio in use — a unified content supply chain story.

Marketing operations maturity

A MOps function driving standardisation — a strong adoption signal.

Red Flags

Small team / simple task tracking

A handful of users tracking tasks — Asana, Monday, or Trello win on simplicity and cost.

Engineering-only use case

Software development workflows — Jira is the better fit.

Low change-management appetite

No willingness to standardise processes — Workfront adoption will struggle.

Budget below enterprise threshold

Too few users to justify enterprise work-management pricing.

No executive sponsor

Work-management transformation needs top-down sponsorship — flag the risk.

Competitive Alternatives

Asana

Wins on ease-of-use and quick adoption for smaller teams. Workfront wins on enterprise scale, governance, and Adobe integration.

Monday.com

Flexible and visual for mid-market. Workfront wins on resource management and complex enterprise workflows.

Smartsheet

Spreadsheet-centric work tracking. Workfront wins on proofing, planning, and creative workflows.

Wrike

Mid-market work management. Workfront wins on enterprise depth and the Adobe creative stack.

Jira

Best for software engineering. Workfront wins for marketing, creative, and cross-functional work.

ServiceNow SPM

Strong in IT and PPM. Workfront wins for marketing work management and Creative Cloud integration.
